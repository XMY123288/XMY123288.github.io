Explanation of rational numbers, irrational numbers, natural numbers, and integers

Rational Numbers

A rational number is any number that can be expressed as the quotient or fraction *p/q* of two integers, where *p* (the numerator) and *q* (the denominator) are integers and *q* is not zero. This is because division by zero is undefined. Since integers can be written as a fraction with a denominator of 1 (e.g., 5 = 5/1), all integers are also rational numbers. Rational numbers can be positive, negative, or zero.

Irrational Numbers

An irrational number is a real number that cannot be expressed as a simple fraction of two integers. Its decimal expansion is non-terminating and non-repeating, meaning it goes on forever without settling into a permanent repeating pattern.

Natural Numbers

Natural numbers are the set of positive integers used for counting and ordering. In mathematics, this set is often denoted as N. The most common definition includes the numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, and so on, extending infinitely. However, there is some debate; some definitions include zero (0) as a natural number, particularly in set theory and computer science.

Integers

Integers encompass all whole numbers, including both positive and negative whole numbers, as well as zero. This set is denoted by the letter Z (from the German word Zahlen, meaning "numbers"). The integers can be broken down into three groups: the positive natural numbers (1, 2, 3,...), their negative counterparts (-1, -2, -3,...), and zero (0). They do not include fractions or decimals. The integers are a subset of the rational numbers.
